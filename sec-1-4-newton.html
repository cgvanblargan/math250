    Section 1.4.7 Newton’s Law of Cooling

    Another classic application of separable equations comes from a simple physical observation: the rate at which an object cools is proportional to the difference between its temperature and the temperature of the surrounding environment. This is known as Newton’s law of cooling. After this, you’ll be able to figure out exactly how long until your coffee goes cold!
    🔗

    Fact 1.4.1. Newton’s Law of Cooling.

    Let \(T(t)\) be the temperature of an object at time \(t\text{,}\) and let \(A\) be the (constant) temperature of the surrounding environment. Then
    \begin{equation*} \frac{dT}{dt} = -k(T - A), \end{equation*}
    where \(k > 0\) is a constant that depends on the object and its surroundings.
    🔗
    🔗
    Notice that this equation is separable. Do you see it’s similarity to the ODE \(\frac{dy}{dt}=ky \text{?}\) You should notice a lot of similarities when solving it, as well! Let’s work through a concrete example. We have broken in it up to a lot of steps in the activity, so make sure to refer to the conclusion afterwards to get a summary of the main points.
    🔗

    Activity 1.4.1. Practice: The Cooling Pizza.

    A pizza is taken out of a 400°F oven and placed in a room that is 60°F. After 20 minutes, the temperature of the pizza is 100°F. Work through the following steps to find \(T(t)\text{,}\) the temperature of the pizza as a function of time. Try each part on your own before checking the solution!
    🔗

    (a) Step 1: Set Up the ODE.

    Which of the following is the correct differential equation for this situation?
    🔗
    • \(\dfrac{dT}{dt} = -k(T - 60)\text{,}\) where \(k > 0\)
    • Correct! The ambient temperature is \(A = 60\)°F, and the negative sign with \(k > 0\) ensures the pizza cools (rather than heats up) when \(T > 60\text{.}\)
    • \(\dfrac{dT}{dt} = k(T - 60)\text{,}\) where \(k > 0\)
    • Close, but the sign is wrong. With \(k > 0\) and no negative sign, this equation says the pizza heats up when \(T > 60\text{,}\) which is the opposite of cooling. We need a negative sign to model decay toward the ambient temperature.
    • \(\dfrac{dT}{dt} = -k(T - 20)\text{,}\) where \(k > 0\)
    • The structure is right but \(A = 20\) is not the ambient temperature here. The room is 60°F, so \(A = 60\text{.}\) The value 20 is the number of minutes, not a temperature.
    • \(\dfrac{dT}{dt} = -k(T - 20)\text{,}\) where \(k < 0\)
    • Two issues here: the ambient temperature should be \(A = 60\)°F, not 20, and Newton’s law of cooling is stated with \(k > 0\) -- the negative sign is already built into the equation. Allowing \(k < 0\) would double the negative and give the wrong behavior.
    🔗

    (b) Step 2: Equilibrium Solution.

    Before solving, what is the equilibrium solution of \(\dfrac{dT}{dt} = -k(T - 60)\text{?}\) If you need a reminder of how to find equilibrium solutions, refer to Section 1.1.5.
    🔗
    • \(T = 60\)
    • Correct! Setting \(\frac{dT}{dt} = 0\) gives \(-k(T - 60) = 0\text{,}\) so \(T = 60\text{.}\) This makes physical sense: when the pizza reaches room temperature, it stops cooling.
    • \(T = 0\)
    • Setting \(\frac{dT}{dt} = 0\) means \(-k(T - 60) = 0\text{.}\) Since \(k \neq 0\text{,}\) we need \(T - 60 = 0\text{,}\) giving \(T = 60\text{,}\) not 0.
    • \(T = 20\)
    • Remember that 20 is the time in minutes, not a temperature. The equilibrium comes from setting \(\frac{dT}{dt} = 0\) and solving for \(T\text{.}\)
    • \(T = 400\)
    • That is the initial temperature of the pizza, not the equilibrium. The equilibrium is where \(\frac{dT}{dt} = 0\text{,}\) meaning the pizza is no longer changing temperature.
    🔗

    (c) Step 3: Separate Variables and Integrate.

    Starting from \(\dfrac{dT}{dt} = -k(T - 60)\text{,}\) separate variables to get
    \begin{equation*} \int \frac{dT}{T - 60} = \int -k \, dt. \end{equation*}
    What do both sides evaluate to?
    🔗
    • \(\ln|T - 60| = -kt + C\)
    • Correct! The left side can be seen as a u-sub with \(u = T - 60\text{,}\) so \(du = dT\text{,}\) giving \(\int \frac{1}{u} \, du = \ln|u| = \ln|T - 60|\text{.}\) Notice that because the coefficient of \(T\) inside is 1, no extra factor appears when substituting -- the integral works out cleanly without any additional adjustment. The right side integrates to \(-kt + C\) as usual.
    • \(\ln(T - 60) = -kt + C\)
    • Almost -- but don’t forget the absolute value signs. We need \(\ln|T - 60|\) since \(T - 60\) could be negative, and the logarithm is only defined for positive inputs.
    • \(\ln|T - 60| = -k + C\)
    • Check the right side. We are integrating \(-k\) with respect to \(t\text{,}\) so the result is \(-kt + C\text{,}\) not just \(-k + C\text{.}\)
    • \((T - 60)^2 = -kt + C\)
    • The left side is not correct. We have \(\int \frac{dT}{T-60}\text{,}\) which is a \(\frac{1}{u}\) integral giving \(\ln|T - 60|\text{,}\) not a power rule integral.
    🔗

    (d) Step 4: Find the General Solution.

    Starting from \(\ln|T - 60| = -kt + C\text{,}\) which of the following is the general solution \(T(t)\text{?}\) This looks very similar to a situation we have seen before -- refer to Checkpoint 1.4.5 for a reminder of how to handle the absolute value and constants.
    🔗
    • \(T(t) = Ce^{-kt} + 60\)
    • Correct! Exponentiating both sides and absorbing \(\pm e^C\) into a single constant \(C\) gives \(T - 60 = Ce^{-kt}\text{,}\) so \(T(t) = Ce^{-kt} + 60\text{.}\)
    • \(T(t) = Ce^{-kt}\)
    • Don’t forget to add the ambient temperature back. After exponentiating we get \(T - 60 = Ce^{-kt}\text{,}\) so we need to add 60 to both sides.
    • \(T(t) = Ce^{kt} + 60\)
    • Check the sign on the exponent. We had \(\ln|T-60| = -kt + C\text{,}\) so exponentiating gives \(e^{-kt}\text{,}\) not \(e^{kt}\text{.}\) A positive exponent would mean the temperature grows without bound, which doesn’t model cooling.
    • \(T(t) = e^{-kt} + C + 60\)
    • The constant \(C\) multiplies the exponential, it does not add to it. When we exponentiate \(\ln|T-60| = -kt + C\) we get \(|T - 60| = e^{-kt + C} = e^C \cdot e^{-kt}\text{,}\) and \(e^C\) becomes a multiplicative constant.
    🔗

    (e) Step 5: Use Initial Conditions to Find \(C\) and \(k\).

    We now have \(T(t) = Ce^{-kt} + 60\text{.}\) From the problem we know:
    \begin{align*} T(0) \amp= 400\\ T(20) \amp= 100. \end{align*}
    Use these values to solve for \(C\) and \(k\text{,}\) and so we will have the final answer!
    🔗
    Solution. Finding the constants to get the final answer
    We first use \(T(0) = 400\) to solve for \(C\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Solution 1. Quick algebra solving for \(C\)
    Plugging in \(t = 0\text{:}\)
    \begin{align*} T(t) \amp= Ce^{t} + 60\\ 400 \amp= Ce^{0} + 60\\ 400 \amp= C + 60\\ C \amp= 340. \end{align*}
    🔗
    🔗
    So we now have:
    \begin{equation*} T(t) = 340e^{-kt} + 60. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Now we use \(T(20) = 100\) to solve for \(k\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Solution 2. Algebra to find \(k\)
    Plugging in \(T(20) = 100\text{:}\)
    \begin{align*} 100 \amp= 340e^{-20k} + 60\\ 40 \amp= 340e^{-20k}\\ \frac{2}{17} \amp= e^{-20k}\\ \ln\!\left(\frac{2}{17}\right) \amp= -20k\\ k \amp= -\frac{1}{20}\ln\!\left(\frac{2}{17}\right) = \frac{\ln(17/2)}{20}. \end{align*}
    Recall what log property we use in the last line. Something similar was done in the radiocarbon dating example!
    🔗
    🔗
    Putting it all together:
    \begin{equation*} T(t) = 340e^{\frac{\ln(2/17)}{20} \cdot t} + 60. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    Let’s summarize everything you just worked through!
    🔗

    Summary of Steps.

    Here is a recap of what we found at each step:
    🔗
    • Step 1 (ODE): The model is \(\frac{dT}{dt} = -k(T - 60)\text{,}\) \(k > 0\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    • Step 2 (Equilibrium): The equilibrium solution is \(T = 60\)
      🔗
      🔗
    • Step 3 (Integrate): Separating and integrating gives \(\ln|T - 60| = -kt + C\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    • Step 4 (General solution): Exponentiating gives the general solution \(T(t) = Ce^{-kt} + 60\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    • Step 5 (Initial conditions): Using \(T(0) = 400\) and \(T(20) = 100\) gives \(C = 340\) and \(k = \frac{\ln(17/2)}{20}\text{,}\) so
      \begin{equation*} T(t) = 340e^{\frac{\ln(2/17)}{20} \cdot t} + 60. \end{equation*}
