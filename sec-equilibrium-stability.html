    Section 2.2.1 Stability of Equilibrium Solutions

    Recall from Section 1.1.5 that an equilibrium solution is a constant solution \(y(t) = C\text{,}\) found by setting \(\dfrac{dy}{dt} = 0\text{.}\) In Section 2.1.2 and Section 2.1.3, we found that both population models had equilibrium solutions at \(P(t) = 0\) and \(P(t) = M\text{.}\) But what made those two pages interesting was not the equilibrium solutions themselves, it was what happened to all the other solutions nearby.
    🔗
    In the logistic model \(\dfrac{dP}{dt} = 4P(5-P)\text{,}\) no matter where a solution started (above or below \(M = 5\)), it converged to \(M\) as \(t \to \infty\text{,}\) as long as \(P(0)>0\text{.}\) The equilibrium at \(P = 5\) acted like a magnet. The equilibrium at \(P = 0\) behaved very differently: solutions that started nearby drifted away from it. We want language to describe this difference.
    🔗
    The figure below shows several solution curves for \(\dfrac{dP}{dt} = 4P(5-P)\) with different initial values. The equilibrium solutions \(P = 0\) and \(P = 5\) are highlighted in green. Note that we include a solution with \(P(0) \lt 0\) as well: while a negative population has no physical meaning, it is still a valid solution to the ODE, and it is useful to see how the equation behaves without restricting to a population context.
    🔗
    We say \(P = 5\) is stable and \(P = 0\) is unstable. Here is the precise definition.
    🔗

    Definition 2.2.2. Stability of Equilibrium Solutions.

    Suppose \(y(t) = C\) is an equilibrium solution to a differential equation. We say \(y(t) = C\) is:
    • stable: if solutions that start near \(C\) converge to \(C\) as \(t \to \infty\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    • unstable: if solutions that start near \(C\) do not converge to \(C\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    • semi-stable: if solutions on one side of \(C\) converge to \(C\text{,}\) but solutions on the other side do not.
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    Now compare this with the model from Section 2.1.3: \(\dfrac{dP}{dt} = -2P(10-P)\text{.}\) The equilibrium solutions are still \(P = 0\) and \(P = 10\text{,}\) but the roles are reversed. Solutions that start below \(M = 10\) decay to zero (extinction), and solutions that start above \(M = 10\) blow up to infinity in finite time (doomsday). Now \(P = 10\) is unstable and \(P = 0\) is stable.
    🔗
    Let’s test your understanding of stability in the context of the doomsday/extinction example from above!
    🔗

    Checkpoint 2.2.4.

    Based on the second graph above, classify the stability of the equilibrium solutions to \(\dfrac{dP}{dt} = -2P(10-P)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    • \(P = 0\) is stable and \(P = 10\) is unstable.
    • Correct! Solutions starting near \(P = 0\) decay toward it, while solutions starting near \(P = 10\) move away — either to extinction or doomsday depending on which side they start.
    • \(P = 0\) is unstable and \(P = 10\) is stable.
    • Not quite — that describes the logistic case from the first graph. Look at what happens to solutions that start near \(P = 10\) in the second graph. Do they converge to 10 or move away from it?
    • Both \(P = 0\) and \(P = 10\) are stable.
    • Not quite. Look at solutions starting just above \(P = 10\) — they blow up to infinity rather than returning to 10.
    • Both \(P = 0\) and \(P = 10\) are unstable.
    • Not quite. Look at solutions starting near \(P = 0\) — they stay close to and converge toward zero over time.
