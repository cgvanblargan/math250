    Section 1.3.1 Slope Fields

    So far we have been solving differential equations and writing down explicit formulas for solutions. That is powerful, but it is not always possible. Many differential equations are too complicated to solve by hand, and even when we can solve them, the formula alone does not always make it easy to picture what the solutions actually look like. In this section we develop a visual tool that lets us understand the behavior of solutions to a differential equation without solving it at all.
    🔗
    The idea starts with something simple. If we have a differential equation \(\dfrac{dy}{dx} = F(x, y)\text{,}\) then at any point \((x, y)\) in the plane, the equation tells us exactly what slope a solution passing through that point must have. We do not need to know the solution to know its slope. We just plug in \(x\) and \(y\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Recall that the general solution to \(\dfrac{dy}{dx} = 2y\) is \(y = Ce^{2x}\text{.}\) Let’s focus on one specific solution, \(y = e^{2x}\) (that is, \(C = 1\)). Since \(\dfrac{dy}{dx} = 2y\text{,}\) the slope of the tangent line to this curve at any point is just twice the \(y\)-value at that point. Use the interactive below to explore this. Try changing both the solution curve (via \(C\)) and the \(y\)-value to see how the slope responds.
    🔗
    Here is the key observation from the interactive above: the slope \(\dfrac{dy}{dx} = 2y\) depends only on the \(y\)-value, not on the specific solution curve or the \(x\)-value. This means that at every point \((x, y)\) in the plane with the same \(y\)-coordinate, the slope of any solution passing through that point is the same.
    🔗
    This gives us a powerful idea. Even without knowing the solutions explicitly, we can draw a short line segment at every point \((x, y)\) with slope equal to \(2y\text{,}\) just from knowing the \(y\)-value. The resulting picture is called a slope field.
    🔗

    Definition 1.3.2. Slope Field.

    A slope field for a differential equation \(\dfrac{dy}{dx} = F(x, y)\) is a plot of short line segments drawn at a grid of points \((x, y)\text{,}\) each with slope equal to \(F(x, y)\text{.}\) Together, these segments show the slope that any solution curve must have as it passes through each point.
    🔗
    🔗
    Let’s build the slope field for \(\dfrac{dy}{dx} = 2y\) step by step. For each \(y\)-value, the slope is \(2y\) regardless of \(x\text{,}\) so every tick in the same row has the same slope. Press play to watch the slope field get drawn.
    🔗
    In practice, computers generate slope fields with far more points than we could draw by hand. The interactive below shows a dense slope field for \(\dfrac{dy}{dx} = 2y\text{.}\) Use the slider to set an initial value \(y(0)\text{,}\) then press play to watch the corresponding solution curve trace itself through the field, following the arrows and connecting the dots. This is the fundamental idea: the slope field tells you which way any solution must travel at every point.
