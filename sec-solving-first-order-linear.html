    Section 1.5.2 Solving First-Order Linear ODEs

    We will rely heavily on the product rule, so below is a quick refresher! It also goes into how we specifically use it in this page.
    🔗
    Before diving in, you may want to review the product rule:
    🔗
    Solution 1. Prerequisite: The Product Rule
    If \(f\) and \(g\) are both functions of \(x\text{,}\) then
    \begin{equation*} (f \cdot g)' = f'g + fg'. \end{equation*}
    For example, with \(f(x) = x^2\) and \(g(x) = e^x\text{:}\)
    \begin{equation*} (x^2 e^x)' = 2x e^x + x^2 e^x. \end{equation*}
    Now consider \(f(x) = e^{2x}\) and \(g(x) = y(x)\text{,}\) where \(y\) is a function of \(x\text{:}\)
    \begin{equation*} \left(e^{2x} \cdot y\right)' = 2e^{2x} y + e^{2x} y'. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
    Okay so now we know how to recognize when an equation is a first-order linear ODE, let’s dig into how to actually solve these things! We’ll work through
    \begin{equation*} y' + 2y = x \end{equation*}
    as our guiding example. The steps may seem a bit random at first, but hopefully by the end of this page you’ll see why we did each step. The key idea is to find an integrating factor, which is a function we multiply both sides by that lets us rewrite the left-hand side as the derivative of a product. Once we do that, we can simply integrate both sides and solve for \(y\text{.}\)
    🔗

    Exploration 1.5.1. Solving \(y' + 2y = x\).

    It turns out the integrating factor for this equation is \(e^{2x}\text{.}\) We will see exactly why this is the right choice shortly. For now, let’s multiply both sides by \(e^{2x}\) and see what happens.
    🔗
    \begin{align*} y' + 2y \amp= x\\ e^{2x}(y' + 2y) \amp= e^{2x} \cdot x\\ e^{2x}y' + 2e^{2x}y \amp= xe^{2x} \end{align*}

    (a) Reversing the Product Rule.

    Look closely at the left-hand side: \(e^{2x}y' + 2e^{2x}y\text{.}\) Which of the following is this equal to? You might need to perform the product rule in some of the choices below!
    🔗
    • \(\left(e^{2x} y\right)'\)
    • Yes! By the product rule, \(\left(e^{2x} y\right)' = e^{2x}y' + 2e^{2x}y\text{,}\) which is exactly the left-hand side. We have reversed the product rule, and can now write our equation as
      \begin{equation*} \left(e^{2x} y\right)' = xe^{2x}. \end{equation*}
    • \(\left(e^{2x}\right)' \cdot y'\)
    • Not quite. \(\left(e^{2x}\right)' = 2e^{2x}\text{,}\) so this gives \(2e^{2x}y'\text{,}\) which does not match.
    • \(e^{2x}(y' + y)\)
    • Close, but distributing gives \(e^{2x}y' + e^{2x}y\text{.}\) The coefficient on \(y\) needs to be \(2e^{2x}\text{,}\) not \(e^{2x}\text{.}\)
    • \(\left(e^{2x} + y\right)'\)
    • No -- this is the derivative of a sum, not a product. We need the product rule here.
    🔗

    (b) What is the Next Step?

    We now have
    \begin{equation*} \left(e^{2x} y\right)' = xe^{2x}. \end{equation*}
    The left-hand side is a derivative. What should we do next to solve for \(y\text{?}\)
    🔗
    Make sure to read the solution feedback by clicking the right answer to see how to finish solving for y.
    🔗
    • Integrate both sides with respect to \(x\text{.}\)
    • Exactly. Integrating both sides gives
      \begin{equation*} e^{2x} y = \int xe^{2x}\,dx, \end{equation*}
      and dividing by \(e^{2x}\) yields
      \begin{equation*} y = \frac{\int xe^{2x}\,dx}{e^{2x}}. \end{equation*}
      The integral on the right requires integration by parts -- we will return to computing it fully later in this section.
    • Differentiate both sides with respect to \(x\text{.}\)
    • Differentiating would make things more complicated. Since the left-hand side is already a derivative, we want to undo it by integrating.
    • Divide both sides by \(e^{2x}\text{.}\)
    • Dividing is part of the last step, but we are not quite there yet. The left-hand side is \((e^{2x} y)'\text{,}\) not \(e^{2x} y\text{.}\) We need to integrate first, then divide.
    • Multiply both sides by \(e^{-2x}\text{.}\)
    • Multiplying by \(e^{-2x}\) is equivalent to dividing by \(e^{2x}\text{,}\) which comes after integrating, not before.
    🔗
    Let’s take stock of what just happened, because the structure here is the key to solving any first-order linear ODE.
    🔗
    Step 1. We multiplied both sides by the integrating factor \(e^{2x}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Step 2. This allowed us to recognize the left-hand side as \(\left(e^{2x} y\right)'\) by reversing the product rule.
    🔗
    Step 3. With the left-hand side written as a single derivative, there is no \(y'\) term to deal with separately. We simply integrate both sides and solve for \(y\text{.}\)
    🔗
    The whole game is choosing the integrating factor correctly so that Step 2 works. Let’s see how to do that in general.
    🔗
    🔗
    So do we need to guess and check for an integrating factor every time? Not at all! It turns out that for any first-order linear ODE
    \begin{equation*} \frac{dy}{dx} + P(x)y = Q(x), \end{equation*}
    the integrating factor is always
    \begin{equation*} \rho(x) = e^{\int P(x)\,dx}. \end{equation*}
    This is precisely what is needed to rewrite the left-hand side in the form \(\left(y \cdot \rho(x)\right)'\text{,}\) which is what allows us to integrate both sides and solve for \(y\text{.}\)
    🔗
    If you feel like it now, you can try working through why this is true by multiplying \(\rho(x)\) on both sides of \(\frac{dy}{dx} + P(x)y = Q(x)\) yourself. You can check the justification now, or later after you’ve had more practice with solving these ODEs!
    🔗
    Solution 2. Justification of the Integrating Factor (optional to read now)
    Multiply both sides of \(\frac{dy}{dx} + P(x)y = Q(x)\) by \(\rho(x) = e^{\int P(x)\,dx}\text{:}\)
    \begin{equation*} e^{\int P(x)\,dx}\frac{dy}{dx} + P(x)e^{\int P(x)\,dx}y = e^{\int P(x)\,dx}Q(x). \end{equation*}
    Now look at the left-hand side. By the product rule and the chain rule:
    \begin{equation*} \frac{d}{dx}\!\left(y \cdot e^{\int P(x)\,dx}\right) = e^{\int P(x)\,dx}y' + P(x)e^{\int P(x)\,dx}y, \end{equation*}
    which is exactly the left-hand side. So we can write:
    \begin{equation*} \frac{d}{dx}\!\left(y \cdot e^{\int P(x)\,dx}\right) = e^{\int P(x)\,dx}Q(x). \end{equation*}
    From here, integrate both sides and solve for \(y\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Theorem 1.5.1. Solution of a First-Order Linear ODE.

    The general solution of the first-order linear equation
    \begin{equation*} \frac{dy}{dx} + P(x)y = Q(x) \end{equation*}
    can be found by multiplying both sides by the integrating factor
    \begin{equation*} \rho(x) = e^{\int P(x)\,dx}, \end{equation*}
    which allows the equation to be rewritten as
    \begin{equation*} \frac{d}{dx}\!\left(y \cdot \rho(x)\right) = \rho(x)\,Q(x). \end{equation*}
    Integrating both sides and solving for \(y\) gives the general solution
    \begin{equation*} y = \frac{\displaystyle\int \rho(x)\,Q(x)\,dx}{\rho(x)}. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
    Let’s connect this back to our example \(y' + 2y = x\text{.}\) Here \(P(x) = 2\) and \(Q(x) = x\text{,}\) so the integrating factor is
    \begin{equation*} \rho(x) = e^{\int 2\,dx} = e^{2x}, \end{equation*}
    which is exactly what we used in the exploration above. The general solution takes the form
    \begin{equation*} y = \frac{\int xe^{2x}\,dx}{e^{2x}} = \frac{\frac{x}{2}e^{2x} - \frac{1}{4}e^{2x} + C}{e^{2x}}. \end{equation*}
    where we evaluated \(\int xe^{2x}\,dx\) using integration by parts.
    🔗
    The theorem tells us this approach works for any first-order linear ODE, not just this one. The only thing that can get in our way now is if the integrals that appear are difficult to evaluate!
