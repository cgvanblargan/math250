    1.3 Section 1.3: Slope fields
    1.3.3 Exercises for Section 1.3

    1.3.3.1. Computing Slopes from a Differential Equation.

    Answer.
    \(x\) \(y\) \(\dfrac{dy}{dx} = x - y\)
    \(0\) \(0\) \(0\)
    \(1\) \(0\) \(1\)
    \(0\) \(1\) \(-1\)
    \(2\) \(1\) \(1\)
    \(-1\) \(2\) \(-3\)
    \(1\) \(1\) \(0\)
    Both zero-slope points satisfy \(y = x\text{.}\) Along the line \(y = x\text{,}\) all tick marks in the slope field are horizontal.
    🔗
    🔗

    1.3.3.2. Matching a Slope Field.

    1.3.3.2.b
    Answer.
    Along \(y = 0\text{,}\) the slope of \(\dfrac{dy}{dx} = x - y\) equals \(x\text{,}\) so tick marks should tilt upward to the right (positive \(x\)) and downward to the left (negative \(x\)). Field B has all horizontal ticks along \(y = 0\) (slope zero everywhere there), which eliminates it. Therefore Field A is correct.
    🔗
    🔗

    1.3.3.3. Reading Behavior from a Slope Field.

    1.3.3.3.a
    Answer.
    Increasing — at \(y=1\) the tick marks point upward.
    🔗
    🔗
    1.3.3.3.b
    Answer.
    Decreasing — at \(y=6\) the tick marks point downward.
    🔗
    🔗
    1.3.3.3.c
    Answer.
    Constant — at \(y=4\) the tick marks are flat, so the solution stays at \(y=4\) for all \(x\text{.}\)

    Answer.
    I, II, III, and IV are first-order linear. V and VI are not.
    🔗
    I, II, III, and IV can all be written in the form \(y' + P(t)y = Q(t)\) where \(P\) and \(Q\) depend only on the independent variable -- not on \(y\text{.}\)

    2.1.4.1. Setting Up Population Models.

    2.1.4.1.a
    Answer.
    \begin{equation*} \frac{dP}{dt} = 5P - 2P^2 = P(5 - 2P). \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
    2.1.4.1.b
    Answer.
    \begin{equation*} \frac{dP}{dt} = k_1 P^2 - k_2 P^2 = (k_1 - k_2)P^2. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
    2.1.4.1.c
    Answer.
    \begin{align*} \frac{dP}{dt} \amp= (10 - P)P - 2P = P(10 - P - 2) = P(8 - P). \end{align*}
    This is logistic with \(k = 1\) and \(M = 8\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    2.1.4.2. Long-Term Behavior: Logistic Equation (\(k \gt 0\)).

    2.1.4.2.a
    Answer.
    \(P(t) = \dfrac{12}{2 + 4e^{-12t}}\text{.}\) As \(t \to \infty\text{,}\) \(e^{-12t} \to 0\text{,}\) so \(P(t) \to \dfrac{12}{2} = 6 = M\text{.}\) The population grows and levels off at the carrying capacity.
    🔗
    🔗
    2.1.4.2.b
    Answer.
    \(P(t) = \dfrac{54}{9 - 3e^{-12t}}\text{.}\) As \(t \to \infty\text{,}\) \(e^{-12t} \to 0\text{,}\) so \(P(t) \to \dfrac{54}{9} = 6 = M\text{.}\) The population decreases and levels off at the carrying capacity.
    🔗
    🔗
    2.1.4.2.c
    Answer.
    \(P(t) = 6\) for all \(t\) — this is the equilibrium solution.
    🔗
    🔗

    2.1.4.3. Long-Term Behavior: Doomsday vs Extinction (\(k \lt 0\)).

    2.1.4.3.a
    Answer.
    \(P(t) = \dfrac{12}{2 + 4e^{12t}}\text{.}\) As \(t \to \infty\text{,}\) \(e^{12t} \to \infty\text{,}\) so \(P(t) \to 0\text{.}\) The population goes extinct.
    🔗
    🔗
    2.1.4.3.b
    Answer.
    \(P(t) = \dfrac{54}{9 - 3e^{12t}}\text{.}\) Denominator zero when \(9 - 3e^{12t} = 0 \Rightarrow t = T = \dfrac{\ln 3}{12}\text{.}\) As \(t \to T^-\text{,}\) \(P(t) \to +\infty\text{.}\) This is doomsday.
    🔗
    🔗
    2.1.4.3.c
    Answer.
    \(P(t) = 6\) for all \(t\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    2.2.7.6. A Semi-Stable Population.

    2.2.7.6.a
    Answer.
    Equilibria: \(P = 4\) (semi-stable), \(P = 10\) (unstable).
    🔗
    🔗
    2.2.7.6.b
    Answer.
    • \(P(0) = 7\text{:}\) falls in \(4 \lt P \lt 10\text{,}\) arrow left so \(P(t)\) is decreasing and converges to the semi-stable equilibrium \(P = 4\text{.}\) The colony shrinks but does not die out.
      🔗
      🔗
    • \(P(0) = 2\text{:}\) falls in \(P \lt 4\text{,}\) arrow left so \(P(t)\) is decreasing and moves away from \(P = 4\text{,}\) heading toward \(P = 0\text{.}\) The colony dies out.
      🔗
      🔗
    • The semi-stable equilibrium at \(P = 4\) acts as a threshold: reducing the population to below 4 million guarantees extinction, but reducing it to above 4 million only shrinks the colony to 4 million — it survives. The antibiotic must be strong enough to push the population strictly below this threshold to eliminate the colony entirely.
