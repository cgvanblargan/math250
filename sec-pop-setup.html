    Section 2.1.1 Setting Up Population Models

    We are now going to explore some very interesting applications of differential equations to population modelling. Early in these notes, we looked at many examples involving \(\frac{dP}{dt} = kP\text{,}\) whose general solution is \(P(t) = Ce^{kt}\text{.}\) The idea that a population grows at a rate proportional to its current size is actually quite natural: the more individuals there are, the more births there are, and so the faster the population grows. Every person contributes, and those contributions add up. For example, if each member of a population contributes on average 2 new members per year, the total growth rate is 2 times the population size, giving \(\frac{dP}{dt} = 2P\text{.}\)
    🔗
    But if you think about it a little longer, you will notice something uncomfortable. If \(k \gt 0\text{,}\) then \(P(t) = Ce^{kt}\) grows without bound as \(t \to \infty\text{.}\) Populations do not actually do that. Resources run out, disease spreads more easily in crowded conditions, competition for food increases. In practice, a rising population tends to put pressure on its own birth rate and death rate in ways that slow growth down. The simple model \(\frac{dP}{dt} = kP\) ignores all of that.
    🔗
    So to build more realistic population models, we need to go back to an idea you have already seen. Recall from Section 1.6.1 that for any quantity \(x(t)\text{,}\)
    \begin{equation*} \frac{dx}{dt} = \text{rate in} - \text{rate out}. \end{equation*}
    For a self-contained population with no migration, the rate in comes entirely from births and the rate out comes entirely from deaths. That gives us the following principle.
    🔗

    Principle 2.1.1. A General Population Model.

    Let \(P(t)\) be the size of a population at time \(t\text{,}\) let \(\beta\) be the birth rate (rate of births per person), and let \(\alpha\) be the death rate (rate of deaths per person). Then
    \begin{equation*} \frac{dP}{dt} = \beta P - \alpha P = (\beta - \alpha)P. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
    It is worth pausing on why \(\beta\) and \(\alpha\) get multiplied by \(P\text{.}\) The rates \(\beta\) and \(\alpha\) are rates per person. So the total number of births per unit time is not just \(\beta\text{,}\) it is \(\beta\) times the number of people contributing to those births, which is \(P\text{.}\) Same logic for deaths. If a town of 1000 people has a birth rate of 0.02 births per person per year, that is \(0.02 \times 1000 = 20\) births per year, not just 0.02. This is the step that trips people up most often, so keep it in mind as you work through the examples below.
    🔗
    Let’s get some practice setting up population models. For each of the following, \(P(t)\) is a population function. Try each one before peeking at the solution.
    🔗

    Activity 2.1.1. Practice: Setting Up Population Models.

    (a)

    The population has a constant birth rate of 2 and its death rate is 0. Write a differential equation for \(P(t)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Hint.
    Recall:
    • The population model: \(\frac{dP}{dt} = \beta P - \alpha P\)
      🔗
      🔗
    • \(\beta = 2\) is the birth rate per person, \(\alpha = 0\) is the death rate per person.
      🔗
      🔗
    Remember: because \(\beta\) is a rate per person, the total birth contribution is \(\beta \cdot P\text{,}\) not just \(\beta\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    Solution.
    Using \(\frac{dP}{dt} = \beta P - \alpha P\) with \(\beta = 2\) and \(\alpha = 0\text{:}\)
    \begin{equation*} \frac{dP}{dt} = 2P - 0 = 2P. \end{equation*}
    Even though the birth rate 2 looks like a plain constant, it is a rate per person, so the total number of births per unit time is \(2 \cdot P\text{.}\) That is why we multiply by \(P\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    (b)

    The birth rate and death rate are both proportional to \(P\text{,}\) with proportionality constants 3 and 4 respectively. Write a differential equation for \(P(t)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Hint.
    Recall:
    • The population model: \(\frac{dP}{dt} = \beta P - \alpha P\)
      🔗
      🔗
    • \(\beta = 3P\) is the birth rate per person, \(\alpha = 4P\) is the death rate per person.
      🔗
      🔗
    Remember: because these are rates per person, each one still gets multiplied by \(P\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    Solution.
    With \(\beta = 3P\) and \(\alpha = 4P\text{:}\)
    \begin{equation*} \frac{dP}{dt} = (3P)P - (4P)P = 3P^2 - 4P^2 = -P^2. \end{equation*}
    Notice that both rates were proportional to \(P\text{,}\) and since each rate per person still gets multiplied by another factor of \(P\text{,}\) we end up with \(P^2\) terms.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    (c)

    The birth rate is proportional to \(P\) with constant \(k_1\text{,}\) and the death rate is proportional to \(P^2\) with constant \(k_2\text{.}\) Which of the following is the correct differential equation for \(P(t)\text{?}\)
    🔗
    • \(\dfrac{dP}{dt} = k_1 P - k_2 P^2\)
    • Not quite. Remember that \(\beta\) and \(\alpha\) are rates per person, so each one gets multiplied by \(P\text{.}\) Here \(\beta = k_1 P\text{,}\) so the birth contribution is \((k_1 P) \cdot P = k_1 P^2\text{,}\) not just \(k_1 P\text{.}\)
    • \(\dfrac{dP}{dt} = k_1 P^2 - k_2 P^3\)
    • Correct! With \(\beta = k_1 P\) and \(\alpha = k_2 P^2\text{:}\)
      \begin{equation*} \frac{dP}{dt} = (k_1 P) \cdot P - (k_2 P^2) \cdot P = k_1 P^2 - k_2 P^3. \end{equation*}
      Each rate per person gets multiplied by \(P\text{,}\) bumping the exponents up by one.
    • \(\dfrac{dP}{dt} = k_1 P^2 - k_2 P^2\)
    • Not quite. The death rate per person is \(k_2 P^2\text{,}\) which when multiplied by \(P\) gives \(k_2 P^3\text{,}\) not \(k_2 P^2\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    Now let’s think about what a more realistic model might look like. In the simple model \(\frac{dP}{dt} = (\beta - \alpha)P\text{,}\) both \(\beta\) and \(\alpha\) were constants. But in reality, as a population grows, resources become scarcer and conditions become more crowded. A natural way to capture this is to let the birth rate decrease as the population increases. Suppose we model the birth rate as
    \begin{equation*} \beta = \beta_1 - \beta_2 P, \quad \beta_1, \beta_2 \gt 0, \end{equation*}
    so that \(\beta\) is high when the population is small and decreases as \(P\) grows. Keeping \(\alpha\) constant and plugging in:
    \begin{align*} \frac{dP}{dt} \amp= (\beta_1 - \beta_2 P)P - \alpha P\\ \amp= P(\beta_1 - \beta_2 P - \alpha)\\ \amp= \beta_2 P\!\left(\frac{\beta_1 - \alpha}{\beta_2} - P\right). \end{align*}
    Setting \(k = \beta_2\) and \(M = \frac{\beta_1 - \alpha}{\beta_2}\text{,}\) this becomes the famous logistic equation.
    🔗

    Definition 2.1.2. The Logistic Equation.

    The logistic equation is
    \begin{equation*} \frac{dP}{dt} = kP(M - P), \end{equation*}
    where \(M\) is called the carrying capacity of the population. It is a common model of population growth and the spread of disease. It is standard to require \(k \gt 0\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Note: solving the logistic equation requires partial fractions, since you will encounter integrals involving \(\dfrac{1}{P(M-P)}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    In the pages ahead, we are going to explore both the case \(k \gt 0\) and \(k \lt 0\) and find some surprisingly interesting behavior in each one.
