    Section 1.1.3 Setting Up Differential Equations

    Recall that in our very first example, we set up the ODE \(\frac{dP}{dt} = 3P\) by:
    • Interpreting "the rate of change of the population" as the derivative \(\frac{dP}{dt}\text{.}\)
      🔗
    • Writing \(3P\) as being proportional to the population \(P\) with proportionality constant of 3.
      🔗
    \begin{equation*} \underbrace{\frac{dP}{dt}}_{\text{rate of change of population}} = \underbrace{3P}_{\text{proportional to } P \text{ with constant 3}} \end{equation*}
    Translating a verbal description into a derivative expression is the key to setting up any ODE.
    🔗
    Note that when a proportionality constant is not specified, we introduce a constant, such as \(k\text{,}\) to represent the unspecified constant of proportionality (as opposed to a specific number like 3 in the population example above).
    🔗

    Activity 1.1.1. Setting Up Differential Equations.

    For each of the following, translate the verbal description into a differential equation. Try each one on your own before checking the solution!
    🔗

    (a)

    The rate of change of a velocity \(v\) of a car is proportional to the cube of \(v\text{.}\) Which of the following correctly models this situation?
    🔗
    • \(\frac{dv}{dt} = kv^3\)
    • Correct! The rate of change of \(v\) is \(\frac{dv}{dt}\text{,}\) and proportional to the cube of \(v\) means the RHS is \(kv^3\text{.}\)
    • \(v = 3v^3\)
    • Not quite — "the rate of change of \(v\)" means we need a derivative on the left-hand side, not just \(v\) itself.
    • \(\frac{dv}{dt} = v^3\)
    • Almost — but since the proportionality constant is unspecified, we need a \(k\) out front. So \(\frac{dv}{dt} = kv^3\text{.}\)
    • \(\frac{dv}{dt} = k\sqrt[3]{v}\)
    • Not quite — be careful with the wording! "The cube of \(v\)" means \(v^3\text{,}\) not the cube root \(\sqrt[3]{v}\text{.}\)
    🔗

    (b)

    Newton’s law of cooling states that the time rate of change of a temperature \(T\) of an object is proportional to the difference between \(T\) and the surrounding temperature \(A\text{.}\) Set up the ODE.
    🔗
    Hint.
    Two key quantities appear in this problem:
    • The rate of change of the temperature of the object is \(\frac{dT}{dt}\text{.}\)
      🔗
    • The difference between the temperature of the object and the surrounding temperature is \(T - A\text{.}\)
      🔗
    These two quantities must be proportional to each other!
    🔗
    🔗
    Solution.
    The ODE is:
    \begin{equation*} \underbrace{\frac{dT}{dt}}_{\text{rate of change of } T} = \underbrace{k(T - A)}_{\text{proportional to the difference } (T-A)} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    The derivative \(\frac{dy}{dx}\) of a function \(y = y(x)\) can also be understood visually: it gives the slope of the graph of the function at each point \((x, y)\) on the curve. More precisely, the tangent line at a point \((x, y)\) is the line passing through \((x, y)\) with slope \(\frac{dy}{dx}\text{.}\) As we move along the curve, the slope of the tangent line changes, and \(\frac{dy}{dx}\) captures exactly how it changes.
    🔗
    Now use this idea about tangent lines to set-up the ODE in the following example!
    🔗

    Example 1.1.2. Setting Up an ODE from a Geometric Description.

    The slope of the tangent line of a graph of \(y(x)\) at the point \((x, y)\) is equal to the square root of the product of \(x\) and \(y\text{.}\) Set up the ODE.
    🔗
    Hint.
    The slope of the tangent line to \(y(x)\) at the point \((x, y)\) is \(\frac{dy}{dx}\text{.}\) This is your left-hand side!
    🔗
    🔗
    Solution.
    \begin{equation*} \frac{dy}{dx} = \sqrt{xy}. \end{equation*}
    The left-hand side \(\frac{dy}{dx}\) is the slope of the tangent line, and the right-hand side \(\sqrt{xy}\) is the square root of the product of \(x\) and \(y\text{.}\)
