    Section 2.2.3 Analyzing Stability with Phase Diagrams

    We now develop a tool for classifying the stability of equilibrium solutions without having to solve the ODE explicitly. Some of what we say here will be justified more carefully in the next section — for now, let’s build intuition through an example.
    🔗
    Consider \(\dfrac{dP}{dt} = 2P(10 - P)\text{,}\) which has equilibrium solutions \(P = 0\) and \(P = 10\text{.}\) These two equilibria divide the \(P\)-axis into three regions.
    🔗
    The figure below shows the solution curves with each region labeled. Notice that solutions starting in a region stay in that region — they get trapped between the equilibrium solutions and can only increase or decrease monotonically. We will justify this precisely in the next section.
    🔗
    In each region, the sign of \(\dfrac{dP}{dt}\) tells us whether solutions are increasing or decreasing.
    🔗
    We can capture all of this information in a compact diagram called a phase diagram. We draw the \(P\)-axis, mark the equilibrium solutions as points, and draw arrows pointing right where \(dP/dt \gt 0\) (solutions increasing) and arrows pointing left where \(dP/dt \lt 0\) (solutions decreasing).
    🔗
    From the directions of the arrows, we can read off the stability directly. At \(P = 10\text{:}\) arrows on both sides point toward it — solutions approaching from below are increasing toward 10, and solutions above are decreasing toward 10. So \(P = 10\) is stable. At \(P = 0\text{:}\) the arrow to the left points away, and the arrow to the right also points away (solutions between 0 and 10 increase, moving away from 0). So \(P = 0\) is unstable.
    🔗

    Activity 2.2.1. Phase Diagram for \(\dfrac{dP}{dt} = 2P(P-10)\).

    Let’s work through the phase diagram method step by step for \(\dfrac{dP}{dt} = 2P(P - 10)\text{.}\)
    🔗

    (a) Step 1: Find the equilibrium solutions.

    The equilibrium solutions are \(P = 0\) and \(P = 10\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Solution. Work
    Set \(2P(P-10) = 0\text{.}\) Then \(P = 0\) or \(P - 10 = 0\text{,}\) giving \(P = 0\) and \(P = 10\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    (b) Step 2: Determine the sign of \(dP/dt\) in each region.

    The equilibria divide the \(P\)-axis into three regions. The sign of \(2P(P-10)\) in each:
    • \(P \lt 0\text{:}\) \(dP/dt \gt 0\) (solutions increasing).
      🔗
      🔗
    • \(0 \lt P \lt 10\text{:}\) \(dP/dt \lt 0\) (solutions decreasing).
      🔗
      🔗
    • \(P \gt 10\text{:}\) \(dP/dt \gt 0\) (solutions increasing).
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    Solution. Sign analysis
    Pick a test value in each region and evaluate \(2P(P-10)\text{:}\)
    • \(P = -1\text{:}\) \(2(-1)(-11) = 22 \gt 0\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    • \(P = 5\text{:}\) \(2(5)(-5) = -50 \lt 0\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    • \(P = 15\text{:}\) \(2(15)(5) = 150 \gt 0\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    Or use sign analysis: \(2P(P-10)\) has factors \(2P\) and \((P-10)\text{.}\) The signs flip at \(P = 0\) and \(P = 10\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    (c) Step 3: Draw the phase diagram.

    Based on the sign analysis above, here is the phase diagram for \(\dfrac{dP}{dt} = 2P(P-10)\text{:}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    (d) Step 4: Determine stability.

    From the phase diagram: \(P = 0\) is stable and \(P = 10\) is unstable.
    🔗
    Solution. Reading stability from the arrows
    At \(P = 0\text{:}\) the arrow to the left points right (toward 0), and the arrow to the right points left (toward 0). Both arrows converge toward \(P = 0\text{,}\) so it is stable.
    🔗
    At \(P = 10\text{:}\) the arrow to the left points left (away from 10), and the arrow to the right points right (away from 10). Both arrows diverge away from \(P = 10\text{,}\) so it is unstable.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Activity 2.2.2. Phase Diagram for \(\dfrac{dy}{dx} = (y-2)^2\).

    Now let’s try an example with a twist.
    🔗

    (a) Step 1: Find the equilibrium solutions.

    The only equilibrium solution is \(y = 2\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Solution. Work
    Set \((y-2)^2 = 0\text{,}\) giving \(y = 2\) as the only solution.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    (b) Step 2: Determine the sign of \(dy/dx\) in each region.

    The equilibrium \(y = 2\) divides the \(y\)-axis into two regions. The sign of \((y-2)^2\) in each:
    • \(y \lt 2\text{:}\) \(dy/dx \gt 0\) (solutions increasing).
      🔗
      🔗
    • \(y \gt 2\text{:}\) \(dy/dx \gt 0\) (solutions increasing).
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    Solution. Sign analysis
    Since \((y-2)^2 \geq 0\) for all \(y\text{,}\) the derivative is always non-negative. It equals zero only at \(y = 2\text{.}\) So solutions are increasing on both sides of the equilibrium.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    (c) Step 3: Draw the phase diagram.

    Based on the sign analysis, here is the phase diagram for \(\dfrac{dy}{dx} = (y-2)^2\text{:}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    (d) Step 4: Determine stability.

    What is the stability of \(y = 2\text{?}\)
    🔗
    • Stable
    • Not quite. Look at the arrows on both sides of \(y = 2\) in the phase diagram. Solutions below \(y = 2\) are increasing toward it, but what about solutions above?
    • Unstable
    • Not quite. Solutions below \(y = 2\) are increasing and do converge toward it — so it is not fully unstable either.
    • Semi-stable
    • Correct! Both arrows point right. Solutions starting below \(y = 2\) increase toward it (converging), but solutions starting above \(y = 2\) also increase — moving away from it (diverging). One side converges, the other diverges, so \(y = 2\) is semi-stable.
    🔗
