    Section 1.6.2 Mixing Problem: Constant Volume

    Before we tackle that beast of a mixing problem we previewed earlier, let’s start with a simpler one. We will use the principle \(\frac{dx}{dt} = \text{rate in} - \text{rate out}\text{.}\) The only tricky part will be using the information we are given (the rate water leaves and the current amount of salt in the tank) to figure out the rate salt leaves.
    🔗

    Example 1.6.1. Mixing Problem with Constant Volume and Pure Water Entering.

    There is a tank with 100 gallons of water that initially contains 20 kg of salt. Pure water is pumped into the tank at 5 gal/min. The mixture is kept uniform by stirring, and the (salty) water leaves the tank at 5 gal/min, thereby keeping the volume of water constant at 100 gallons. Suppose \(x(t)\) is the amount of salt in the tank (in kg) as a function of time \(t\) in minutes. Set up a differential equation and solve for \(x(t)\text{.}\)
    🔗

    (a) Part 1: Rate Salt Enters the Tank.

    Pure water is pumped in at 5 gal/min. What is the rate at which salt enters the tank?
    🔗
    Hint.
    Don’t over think it! How much salt is in pure water?
    🔗
    🔗
    Solution.
    Since pure water is entering, there is no salt in the incoming water. So the rate salt enters is
    \begin{equation*} \text{rate in} = 0 \text{ kg/min}. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    (b) Part 2: Rate Salt Leaves the Tank.

    We need to use the next two pieces of information for the rate out:
    • Water leaves the tank at 5 gal/min.
      🔗
      🔗
    • The volume of water in the tank remains at 100 gallons (as the water entering = water leaving)
      🔗
      🔗
    What is the rate at which salt leaves the tank? This will take more work, and the answer will also involve \(x(t)\text{,}\) the quantity of salt currently in the tank!
    🔗
    Hint.
    The rate salt leaves is not simply 5 gal/min -- that is the rate water leaves. To get the rate salt leaves, think about the concentration of salt in the tank:
    \begin{equation*} \text{rate salt leaves} = \text{concentration of salt in tank} \times \text{rate water leaves}. \end{equation*}
    The concentration is the amount of salt divided by the volume of water.
    🔗
    🔗
    Solution.
    The concentration of salt in the tank at time \(t\) is \(\dfrac{x(t)}{100}\) kg/gal, and this is the stuff leaving the tank! Multiplying by the flow rate out for water gives the total rate out of salt:
    \begin{equation*} \text{rate out} = \underbrace{\frac{x(t)}{100}}_{\text{salt concentration}} \times \underbrace{5}_{\text{flow rate of water}} \text{ kg/min}, \end{equation*}
    which simplifies to \(\dfrac{x(t)}{20}\) kg/min.
    🔗
    Solution. Unit check
    \begin{equation*} \frac{x(t) \text{ kg salt}}{100 \text{ gal water}} \cdot \frac{5 \text{ gal}}{\text{min}} = \frac{5x(t)}{100} \frac{\text{kg}}{\text{min}} = \frac{x(t)}{20} \frac{\text{kg}}{\text{min}}. \checkmark \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    (c) Part 3: The ODE and Solution.

    The hard work is done! We have identified rate in and rate out. Combining them:
    \begin{equation*} \frac{dx}{dt} = \underbrace{0}_{\text{rate in}} - \underbrace{\frac{x}{20}}_{\text{rate out}} = -\frac{x}{20}. \end{equation*}
    Apply the initial condition \(x(0) = 20\) (the initial amount of salt in the tank) and solve for \(x(t)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    The meat of the problem is done, and there is nothing new involved in actually solving the ODE -- this is just a standard separable ODE (it is also first-order linear, so you can use either method!). For completeness, the solution is below.
    🔗
    Solution. Solving the ODE (can skim or skip for now)
    Separating and integrating:
    \begin{align*} \frac{dx}{x} \amp= -\frac{1}{20}\,dt\\ \ln|x| \amp= -\frac{t}{20} + C\\ x(t) \amp= Ce^{-t/20}. \end{align*}
    Applying the initial condition \(x(0) = 20\text{:}\)
    \begin{equation*} 20 = Ce^{0} \implies C = 20. \end{equation*}
    So the solution is \(x(t) = 20e^{-t/20}.\)
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Key Takeaways.

    The key insight was computing the rate out carefully:
    \begin{align*} \text{rate salt leaves} \amp= \text{(salt concentration in tank)} \times \text{(rate water leaves)}\\ \text{salt concentration} \amp= \frac{\text{amount of salt in tank}}{\text{volume of tank}} = \frac{x(t)}{V(t)} \end{align*}
    where in this problem \(V(t) = 100\) gallons (constant). A similar idea applies to the rate salt is entering, but the rate in was simply 0 as only pure water was coming in. In the next page, we tackle a harder version where the volume is no longer constant and salty water is entering, which makes both the setup and the solving more involved.
