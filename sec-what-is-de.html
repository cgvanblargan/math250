    Section 1.1.1 What is a Differential Equation?

    Derivatives are used to model changing situations! And things are changing all around us!
    🔗

    Example 1.1.2. Setting Up a Differential Equation.

    Come up with an equation that models the following situation: The rate of change of a population \(P(t)\) (where \(t\) is time in years) is proportional to the current population. The constant of proportionality is 3.
    🔗
    Hint.
    The rate of change of \(P\) is the derivative with respect to time, which is \(\frac{dP}{dt}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    "The rate of change of \(P(t)\) is..." translates to \(\frac{dP}{dt} = \ldots\) What can you put on the right-hand side of the equation to represent that it is proportional to the current population, where the constant of proportionality is 3?
    🔗
    🔗
    Solution.
    The answer is \(\frac{dP}{dt} = 3P\text{.}\) The left-hand side \(\frac{dP}{dt}\) denotes the rate of change of the population. The right-hand side, \(3P\text{,}\) expresses a quantity that is proportional to \(P\text{,}\) with a proportionality constant of 3.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    In the previous example, you just set up a differential equation. We often want to take this a step further and find a solution. In this case, that would be a population function \(P(t)\) that satisfies the conditions of the previous example (where the rate of change of the population is proportional to itself, with proportionality constant equaling 3).
    🔗

    Example 1.1.3. Solving a Differential Equation.

    Can you think of a function \(P(t)\) that solves \(\frac{dP}{dt} = 3P\text{?}\)
    🔗
    Hint.
    First try solving the simpler question: find a function \(P(t)\) whose derivative is itself, that is, satisfying \(\frac{dP}{dt} = P\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    Solution.
    Let’s start with the simpler equation \(\frac{dP}{dt} = P\text{.}\) Can you think of a function whose derivative is itself?
    🔗
    Solution 1. Reveal
    Recall that \(P(t) = e^t\) has this property — its derivative is itself! So \(P(t) = e^t\) satisfies \(\frac{dP}{dt} = P\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    Now, how can we modify this to solve \(\frac{dP}{dt} = 3P\text{?}\) We need a function whose derivative produces a factor of 3 out front. Think about the chain rule!
    🔗
    Solution 2. Reveal
    Let’s check \(P(t) = e^{3t}\text{.}\) By the chain rule:
    \begin{equation*} \frac{d}{dt}e^{3t} = 3\underbrace{e^{3t}}_{=\,P} = 3P. \end{equation*}
    So \(P(t) = e^{3t}\) solves \(\frac{dP}{dt} = 3P\text{.}\) Can you think of any other solutions?
    🔗
    🔗
    Note that there are other solutions besides the ones we just stated! We discuss this in the next section.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    The population example illustrates a key idea: when modelling situations that change over time, we naturally arrive at equations involving derivatives. This is the essence of a differential equation, which we now define formally.
    🔗

    Definition 1.1.4. Differential Equation.

    A differential equation is an equation involving a function \(y(x)\text{,}\) its derivatives \(y', y'', y''', \ldots\text{,}\) and an independent variable \(x\text{.}\)
    🔗
    The order of a differential equation is the highest derivative appearing.
    🔗
    🔗
    A differential equation involving only derivative with respect to only one variable (as opposed to partial derivatives) is called an ordinary differential equation (ODE).
    🔗
    Here are three examples of ODEs, along with their orders:
    \begin{align*} \frac{dP}{dt} \amp= 3P \quad \leadsto \text{1st order}\\ \frac{d^2y}{dx^2} + x^2 \amp= \frac{dy}{dx} e^x \quad \leadsto \text{2nd order}\\ y''' + y''y + \sin(y') \amp= x \quad \leadsto \text{3rd order} \end{align*}
    🔗

    Remark 1.1.5. Notation.

    We adopt both the prime notation \(y'\) and Leibniz notation \(\frac{dy}{dt}\) to denote the derivative of \(y\) with respect to \(t\text{.}\) These are equivalent and will be used interchangeably throughout.
    🔗
    In \(\frac{dP}{dt} = 3P\text{,}\) \(t\) is the independent variable and \(P\) is the dependent variable. The actual letters we use for the variable will vary. In the last example, \(y''' + y''y + \sin(y') = x\text{,}\) it is implied that \(y\) is the dependent variable and \(x\) is the independent variable.
