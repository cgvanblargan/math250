    Section 1.6.3 Mixing Problem: Changing Volume

    We are finally ready to revisit the beast of a problem from the intro page! There is quite a bit to parse, and the first time through a problem like this takes some patience. But the main patterns repeat, and with practice these become routine. After working through the four parts below, make sure to check the Key Takeaways at the end which ties everything together neatly.
    🔗

    Example 1.6.1. Mixing Problem with Changing Volume and Salty Water Entering.

    There is a tank with 100 gallons of water that initially contains 20 kg of salt. Water with 5 kg of salt per gallon is pumped into the tank at 10 gal/min. The mixture is kept uniform by stirring, and water leaves the tank at 5 gal/min. Suppose \(x(t)\) is the amount of salt in the tank (in kg) as a function of time \(t\) in minutes. Set up a differential equation and solve for \(x(t)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    As before, \(\frac{dx}{dt} = \text{rate in} - \text{rate out}\text{.}\) Let’s figure out each piece.
    🔗

    (a) Part 1: Rate Salt Enters the Tank.

    This time, salty water is being pumped in -- not pure water! Water with 5 kg of salt per gallon enters at 10 gal/min. What is the rate at which salt enters the tank?
    🔗
    Hint.
    Rate salt enters = (salt concentration of incoming water) × (water flow rate in). You have both pieces of information in the problem statement.
    🔗
    🔗
    Solution.
    The incoming water has a salt concentration of 5 kg/gal, and water flows in at 10 gal/min. So:
    \begin{equation*} \text{rate in} = \underbrace{5}_{\text{salt concentration}} \times \underbrace{10}_{\text{water flow rate in}} = 50 \text{ kg/min}. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    (b) Part 2: Rate Salt Leaves the Tank.

    A big difference in this example is that the volume of water in the tank, \(V(t)\text{,}\) is changing. However, the same principle as in the previous example 1.6.1.b applies. Here is the relevant information:
    🔗
    • Water leaves the tank at 5 gal/min (this is thewaterflow rate out).
      🔗
      🔗
    • \(V(t)\) represents the volume of water in the tank at time \(t\text{,}\) which is currently unknown.
      🔗
      🔗
    Without solving for \(V(t)\) yet, which of the following correctly gives the rate salt leaves the tank?
    🔗
    • \(\dfrac{x(t)}{V(t)} \cdot 5\)
    • Correct! The salt concentration in the tank is \(\dfrac{x(t)}{V(t)}\) kg/gal, and the water flow rate out is 5 gal/min. So rate out = \(\dfrac{x(t)}{V(t)} \cdot 5\) kg/min.
    • \(\dfrac{x(t)}{100} \cdot 5\)
    • This was the rate out in the previous example, where the volume was constant at 100 gallons. Here the volume is changing, so we need \(V(t)\) in place of 100.
    • \(5 \cdot 10\)
    • This is the rate salt enters the tank, not the rate it leaves. The rate out depends on the current concentration of salt in the tank, which involves \(x(t)\text{.}\)
    • \(\dfrac{x(t)}{V(t)} \cdot 10\)
    • Close -- the concentration \(\dfrac{x(t)}{V(t)}\) is right, but the water flow rate out is 5 gal/min, not 10. The 10 gal/min is the flow rate in.
    🔗

    (c) Part 3: Finding \(V(t)\).

    From Part 2, the rate out involves \(V(t)\text{,}\) which we still need to find. Water flows in at 10 gal/min and out at 5 gal/min, with an initial volume of 100 gallons. Find \(V(t)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Hint.
    The same rate in, rate out principle applies to the volume of water:
    \begin{equation*} \frac{dV}{dt} = \text{water flow rate in} - \text{water flow rate out}. \end{equation*}
    Use the initial condition \(V(0) = 100\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    Solution.
    \begin{align*} \frac{dV}{dt} \amp= 10 - 5 = 5\\ V(t) \amp= 5t + C. \end{align*}
    Applying \(V(0) = 100\) gives \(C = 100\text{,}\) so
    \begin{equation*} V(t) = 5t + 100. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    (d) Part 4: The ODE and Solution.

    Putting it all together with \(V(t) = 5t + 100\text{:}\)
    \begin{equation*} \frac{dx}{dt} = \underbrace{50}_{\text{rate in}} - \underbrace{\frac{x(t)}{5t+100} \cdot 5}_{\text{rate out}} = 50 - \frac{x}{t+20}. \end{equation*}
    Rewriting in standard form:
    \begin{equation*} \frac{dx}{dt} + \frac{1}{t+20} x = 50. \end{equation*}
    This is a first-order linear ODE! The hard work is done, the solution to the ODE is below.
    🔗
    Solution. Solving the ODE (can skim or skip for now)
    We have \(P(t) = \dfrac{1}{t+20}\) and \(Q(t) = 50\text{.}\) The integrating factor is:
    \begin{align*} \rho(t) \amp= e^{\int \frac{1}{t+20}\,dt} = e^{\ln(t+20)} = t+20. \end{align*}
    Applying the shortcut:
    \begin{align*} \rho(t) \cdot x \amp= \int \rho(t)\, Q(t)\,dt\\ (t+20)\, x \amp= \int (t+20) \cdot 50\,dt\\ (t+20)\, x \amp= \int (50t + 1000)\,dt\\ (t+20)\, x \amp= 25t^2 + 1000t + C. \end{align*}
    Dividing by \((t+20)\text{:}\)
    \begin{equation*} x = \frac{25t^2 + 1000t + C}{t+20}. \end{equation*}
    Applying \(x(0) = 20\text{:}\)
    \begin{align*} 20 \amp= \frac{0 + 0 + C}{20}\\ C \amp= 400. \end{align*}
    So the solution is
    \begin{equation*} x(t) = \frac{25t^2 + 1000t + 400}{t+20}. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Key Takeaways: Setting Up Mixing Problems.

    The mixing problems on this page and the previous one follow the same fundamental structure. The two rates you always need are:
    \begin{align*} \text{rate in} \amp= \text{(salt concentration of incoming water)} \times \text{(water flow rate in)}\\ \text{rate out} \amp= \underbrace{\frac{x(t)}{V(t)}}_{\text{salt concentration in tank}} \times \text{(water flow rate out)} \end{align*}
    When the volume is changing, you need to find \(V(t)\) first using the same rate in, rate out principle applied to volume -- then plug it in. Once the ODE is set up, it is first-order linear, and you already know how to handle that!
