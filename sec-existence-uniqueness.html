    Section 2.2.4 Consequences of Existence and Uniqueness

    The phase diagram method we developed on the previous page is powerful, but you might be wondering why it actually works. Why does the sign of \(f(y)\) tell us the long-term behavior of solutions? Why can’t a solution just jump from one region to another? The answer comes from a fundamental theorem about autonomous differential equations. We aren’t going to dwell on the theorem itself, but we need to understand how its consequences give us a much cleaner picture of the behavior of solutions and why our stability analysis is reliable.
    🔗

    Theorem 2.2.1. Existence and Uniqueness for First-Order Autonomous DEs.

    Suppose we are solving the first-order autonomous DE \(\dfrac{dy}{dt} = f(y)\text{,}\) where \(f\) and \(f'(y)\) are continuous. Then given any initial value \(y(0) = y_0\text{:}\)
    • A solution exists.
      🔗
      🔗
    • The solution is unique.
      🔗
      🔗
    • Solutions do not intersect.
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    Solution. Optional: full technical statement
    The theorem above is somewhat simplified. The full statement is: suppose \(f(y)\) and \(\dfrac{\partial f}{\partial y}\) are continuous on some open region \(R\) of the \(ty\)-plane containing the point \((t_0, y_0)\text{.}\) Then there exists an interval \((t_0 - h, t_0 + h)\) for some \(h \gt 0\) on which a unique solution to \(\dfrac{dy}{dt} = f(y)\) with \(y(t_0) = y_0\) exists. Since our equation is autonomous (no \(t\) on the right-hand side), we can take \(t_0 = 0\) without loss of generality.
    🔗
    🔗
    The video below walks through the reasoning behind each of the consequences listed here. We will not write out all the reasoning in the notes — what matters most is understanding and applying the results, which we do on this page immediately after the video.
    🔗

    Observation 2.2.3. Consequences of Existence and Uniqueness.

    In a first-order autonomous ODE \(\dfrac{dy}{dt} = f(y)\text{,}\) the following apply to continuous solutions \(y(t)\text{:}\)
    1. A solution that starts at an equilibrium value stays there. If \(y(t) = M\) is an equilibrium solution, then the only solution with \(y(0) = M\) is \(y(t) = M\) itself. No other solution can pass through a point on an equilibrium.
      🔗
      🔗
    2. Solutions grow or decrease forever. Since the sign of \(f(y)\) is constant within each zone between equilibria, and solutions cannot intersect one another, a solution that starts out increasing stays increasing for its entire interval of existence, and similarly for decreasing solutions.
      🔗
      🔗
    3. Solutions are trapped between equilibria. Because solutions are continuous and cannot cross an equilibrium (by consequence 1), a solution that starts between two adjacent equilibria stays between them for its entire interval of existence.
      🔗
      🔗
    4. Convergence to stable equilibria. A solution that is increasing and trapped below an equilibrium \(M\) (with no other equilibrium between them) must converge to \(M\) as \(t \to \infty\text{.}\) Similarly, a decreasing solution trapped above an equilibrium must converge to it.
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    These consequences show that the phase diagram is useful for determining the long-term behavior of solutions. For example, in consequence 2, we see that determining whether a continuous solution is growing or decreasing forever is determined purely by which region \(P(0)\) falls in. For \(\dfrac{dP}{dt} = 2P(P-10)\text{:}\) if \(P(0) \in (-\infty, 0)\) or \(P(0) \in (10, \infty)\text{,}\) then \(P(t)\) grows forever. If \(P(0) \in (0, 10)\text{,}\) then \(P(t)\) decreases forever.
    🔗
    We can also read stability directly from the phase diagram, which relates to consequence 4. If arrows on both sides of an equilibrium point toward it, the equilibrium is stable, meaning nearby solutions converge to it. If arrows on both sides point away, it is unstable. For this example, \(P = 0\) is stable and \(P = 10\) is unstable.
    🔗

    Remark 2.2.6. A Note on "Growing Forever" and the Doomsday Case.

    When we say a solution "grows forever," the doomsday example from Section 2.1.3 deserves a careful look. In that example, the solution with \(P(0) \gt M\) starts above the upper equilibrium and is increasing. The solution remains continuous and stays above \(M\) on its entire interval of existence \([0, T)\text{,}\) and it blows up to infinity as \(t \to T^-\text{.}\) So it does grow forever, but only up to time \(T\text{,}\) and the model breaks down at doomsday.
    🔗
    For \(t \gt T\text{,}\) if we allow discontinuous solutions, the formula continues to give values, but the solution jumps from \(+\infty\) to large negative values at \(T\text{,}\) which is physically meaningless for a population and not a continuous solution. In particular, it is not accurate to say \(P(t) \to \infty\) as \(t \to \infty\text{.}\)
    🔗
    In the stability analysis that follows, we will classify solutions starting above an upper non-stable equilibrium as "growing forever" without distinguishing the finite-time blowup case from true growth to infinity. The phase diagram correctly captures the direction of this behavior either way.
    🔗
    🔗
